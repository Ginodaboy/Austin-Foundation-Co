Slab Foundation Repair · Austin, TX

Slab Foundation Repair in Austin: Push Piers, Helical Piers, and What It Actually Costs

Most homes built in Austin since the 1970s sit on a concrete slab foundation — and Central Texas clay soil is unusually hard on them. Here's how slab repair actually works, what determines the price, and how to tell if your slab needs it.

Updated for 2026 6 min read Reviewed by our inspection team

If your Austin home was built on a concrete slab — which describes the large majority of houses built here since the 1970s — your foundation has no crawl space, no basement, and no give. The slab sits directly on the soil. When that soil expands and contracts, the slab has to absorb the movement, and over time, that shows up as cracks, sloping floors, and doors that no longer close right.

This guide covers how slab foundation repair actually works in Austin specifically, what affects the price, and the warning signs worth taking seriously.

Why Austin slabs move more than you'd expect

Central Texas sits on expansive clay soil — soil that can swell significantly when wet and shrink just as dramatically when dry. Austin's swing between soaking spring rains and bone-dry summer heat means that soil under your slab is constantly expanding and contracting, season to season.

That movement isn't uniform. One corner of your house might sit on soil that's drier (and shrinking more) than another corner that stays moist under a tree canopy or a leaking gutter downspout. That uneven movement is what actually cracks a slab — not the soil moving, but the soil moving unevenly.

The two repair methods: push piers vs. helical piers

Almost all modern slab foundation repair uses one of two pier systems, driven down to stable soil or bedrock and used to lift and stabilize the slab.

Push piers (resistance piers)

Push piers are steel tubes hydraulically driven into the ground using the weight of the house itself as resistance, until they reach load-bearing soil or rock — often 15 to 40 feet down in the Austin area. They're typically used on heavier structures, including most single-story and two-story homes.

Helical piers

Helical piers look more like giant screws and are mechanically torqued into the ground rather than driven. They're often the better fit for lighter structures, additions, or sites where access is tight, since they don't require the same reaction load.

Which one do I need?

This depends on your home's weight, soil report, and how deep stable soil sits beneath your specific lot — not a standard answer. This is exactly what a proper inspection determines before any quote is given.

What slab foundation repair costs in Austin

Pricing depends on the number of piers needed, depth required to reach stable soil, and access to the affected area. Here's a general range based on typical Austin-area jobs:

Minor crack injection (1–2 cracks)$450 – $1,800

These are general ranges, not quotes — the only way to get an accurate number for your home is a free inspection, since pier count and depth vary lot to lot, even on the same street.

Warning signs your slab may need repair

  • Diagonal cracks above doors and windows — usually the first visible sign of slab movement.
  • Doors or windows that stick, or won't latch like they used to — the frame is racking out of square.
  • Sloping floors — a marble or ball that rolls on its own on a "level" floor is a clear sign.
  • Gaps between baseboards and the floor — suggests the slab has pulled away from the wall framing.
  • Cracks in exterior brick, especially stair-step patterns — these run along mortar joints and are a stronger indicator than drywall cracks alone.

How long does slab repair take?

Most residential pier installations are completed in 1 to 3 days, depending on the number of piers and site access. Crack injection alone is often a same-day repair. Your written quote will include a specific timeline for your home.

Will insurance cover slab repair?

Usually not. Most homeowners insurance policies treat foundation movement from soil conditions as a maintenance issue rather than a covered peril, unless the damage was directly caused by something like a covered plumbing leak. We can help you understand which applies during your inspection.
