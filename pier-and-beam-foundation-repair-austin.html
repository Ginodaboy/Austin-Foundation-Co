Pier & Beam Foundation Repair in Austin: What Older Homes Need to Know

If your home has a crawl space instead of a slab, your foundation works — and fails — differently. Here's how pier and beam repair works, the problems specific to older Austin homes, and what it costs.

Updated for 2026 6 min read Reviewed by our inspection team

Pier and beam foundations are common in Austin homes built before the 1960s, especially in central neighborhoods like Hyde Park, Travis Heights, and Old West Austin. Instead of a concrete slab sitting directly on the ground, the house rests on a system of concrete or wood piers, with wooden beams and joists spanning between them — leaving a crawl space underneath.

That crawl space is both the advantage and the vulnerability. It gives you access to plumbing and wiring without tearing up a slab, but it also means moisture, wood rot, and pier settlement can cause problems a slab home simply doesn't face.

How pier & beam differs from slab foundations

Pier & Beam

Crawl space construction

  • Raised off the ground on piers
  • Accessible crawl space underneath
  • Vulnerable to wood rot & moisture
  • Common in pre-1960s Austin homes
Slab

Concrete-on-grade

  • Sits directly on the soil
  • No crawl space access
  • Vulnerable to soil movement
  • Common in post-1970s Austin homes

Common pier & beam problems in Austin

Wood rot and moisture damage

Central Texas humidity, combined with poor crawl space ventilation, creates ideal conditions for wood rot in floor joists and beams. This is the single most common reason pier and beam homes need repair — and it's frequently invisible until someone is underneath the house looking for it.

Settled or shifted piers

Older concrete or wood piers can settle unevenly as the soil beneath them moves, especially if the original piers weren't set deep enough to reach stable soil — a common shortcut in homes built before modern foundation standards.

Sagging or bouncy floors

If a floor feels noticeably springy when you walk across it, that's often a sign of a weakened or rotted joist rather than a soil issue — a different repair than what a slab home with the same complaint would need.

Why this matters for diagnosis

Pier and beam problems are frequently a wood issue, not a soil issue — which means the fix is often joist and beam replacement, not just adding piers. An inspection that goes underneath the house, not just around the exterior, is essential to get this right.

How pier & beam repair works

  1. Crawl space inspection — a technician physically goes underneath the house to assess pier condition, wood moisture levels, and joist integrity.
  2. Pier replacement or shimming — settled piers are releveled, replaced with concrete piers, or shimmed depending on the extent of movement.
  3. Wood sistering or replacement — rotted joists and beams are reinforced ("sistered" with new lumber alongside the old) or fully replaced if rot is extensive.
  4. Moisture correction — vapor barriers and improved ventilation are often added to prevent the same wood rot from recurring.

How long does pier & beam repair take?

Pier releveling alone is often completed in 1–2 days. Jobs that include significant joist or beam replacement can take 3–5 days depending on the extent of wood damage found once the crawl space is opened up.
